Magic doesn't interact with our world. It lies over it like a fine film of dew. That is — unless you bring alchemy into play.

Magic and alchemy are two contrary forces. They oppose each other by nature, which makes them powerful allies once mastered.

A mage skilled enough can temporarily alter reality — their magic so powerful that its superimposition becomes tangible.

Take an example:

A basic fire-creation spell. Magical fire doesn't burn or consume; it's like a holographic lighter.

Now imagine an alchemist uses their craft to siphon that fire and trade it with the Alternate World. What would happen? This fire isn't from our world, so it has no equivalent in the Alternate World.

The result is that a whole new reality is created in the Alternate World. The world is logical, and when something falls outside its logic, it corrects it — and so it creates an equivalent element. The alchemist thus makes concrete what magic could not before: the alchemical fire burns, far more intensely than normal fire.

And the other way around? Since magic creates and alchemy concretizes. Take another example:

A basic transmutation of iron + copper yields gold. Any self-respecting alchemist knows that. But what happens if, during the transmutation, the mage adds a touch of a newly created element — say, oneiric water (water created by magic)?

The magical element concretizes during the transmutation and creates a new alloy: syllarite (a pale blue, very shiny ore).

That's the whole principle of alchiromancy — drawing on both alchemy and magic at once!
